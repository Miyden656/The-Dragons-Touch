# Example 001 — Phelia, Exuberant Shepherd: “Corgi Butt”

## Quick Summary

This example shows The Dragon’s Touch reviewing a legal 100-card Commander deck led by Phelia, Exuberant Shepherd.

The deck, “Corgi Butt,” is built around a Dog/corgi typal feel with a cute-until-dangerous identity. The pilot wanted the deck to stay close to its theme while still becoming more functional at a casual/core Commander table.

The review was not about forcing the deck into a generic optimized shell. It was about helping the pilot tune the deck without losing what made the deck fun.

## Deck Snapshot

- Commander: Phelia, Exuberant Shepherd
- Deck name: Corgi Butt
- Review type: Optional tuning
- Deck status: Legal 100-card Commander deck
- Required cuts: 0
- Budget/source: Collection-only, $0 outside purchases
- Philosophy lens: Theme / Vibe
- Guide/persona: Bethany, The Theme Mentor
- Target table/power: Bracket 2 casual/core
- Infinite combos: Not desired
- Outside-card recommendations: Not desired unless the pilot chooses to shift away from collection-only

## What the Pilot Wanted

The pilot wanted the deck to feel like a Dog/corgi typal deck first.

The desired feel was:

- Cute until dangerous
- Dog/corgi typal
- Capable of building toward a giant Corgi win condition
- Functional enough to play at a casual/core table
- Not pushed toward infinite combos or high-power optimization

The pilot also wanted recommendations to stay within owned cards only. This made the review a collection-only tuning pass, not a shopping list.

## What the Tool Initially Read

The tool identified the primary strategy as:

- Token Combat / Go-Wide-Go-Tall

It initially identified the secondary strategy as:

- Cast From Outside Hand Value

This was a reasonable automated read based on some card patterns, but it did not fully capture what the deck was actually trying to do in play.

## What the Pilot Corrected

During the guided review, the pilot corrected the secondary strategy to:

- ETB Control / Flicker Control

This correction mattered because Phelia’s role in the deck was not just to support generic value. Phelia was acting as a commander, engine, and identity anchor for a flicker-based control package.

The deck was not simply trying to cast from unusual zones. It was trying to reuse enter-the-battlefield effects, control threats through flicker-style interaction, and preserve a strong Dog/corgi theme while doing so.

## Why the Correction Mattered

This was one of the most important parts of the review.

The Dragon’s Touch allowed the pilot to correct the strategy read instead of locking the deck into the first automated interpretation.

That correction helped the final review better respect:

- Phelia as the commander engine
- Flicker effects as part of the actual game plan
- ETB interaction as a meaningful control package
- The Dog/corgi theme as a real deckbuilding priority
- The pilot’s desired casual/core power level

The result was a more honest review. Instead of treating theme pieces as inefficient by default, the tool could better separate “wrong for the deck” from “low raw power but correct for this deck.”

## What the Tool Protected

The review protected several cards and concepts because they supported the deck’s identity, function, or desired play experience.

Protected cards and concepts included:

- Phelia, Exuberant Shepherd as the commander/engine/identity anchor
- Spirited Companion as a perfect theme + function card
- Cloudshift
- Flicker of Fate
- Slip On the Ring
- Grasp of Fate
- The Princess Takes Flight
- Rogue’s Passage for the giant Corgi win condition
- The flicker/ETB-control package
- The Dog/corgi typal feel

The review also treated Panharmonicon as a playtest card rather than an automatic cut. It may be powerful in the right board state, but it still needs real-game testing to see whether it supports the deck consistently enough.

## Suggested Test Package

These swaps are not presented as perfect or final.

They are a suggested test package for the pilot to try.

### Out

- Soul Tithe
- Divine Gambit
- Sisay’s Ring

### In

- Hanged Executioner
- Odric, Lunarch Marshal
- Ajani, Caller of the Pride

## Why These Swaps Were Suggested

Soul Tithe and Divine Gambit were identified as replaceable interaction pieces that may not consistently support the deck’s corrected plan.

Sisay’s Ring was identified as a possible efficiency cut because the deck could likely use that slot for something more connected to its theme, board development, or win plan.

Hanged Executioner supports token presence, interaction, and creature-based board development.

Odric, Lunarch Marshal supports the deck’s go-wide/go-tall combat finish potential and can help turn a cute board into a dangerous one.

Ajani, Caller of the Pride supports the giant Corgi win condition by helping one creature become a real combat threat.

## Why This Example Matters

This example matters because it shows the kind of review The Dragon’s Touch is trying to provide.

The tool did not simply say, “Here are the strongest cards.”

Instead, it showed a workflow where:

- The deck was already legal, so cuts were optional
- The pilot corrected the strategy read
- Theme was treated as a valid deckbuilding priority
- Collection-only restrictions were respected
- The tool protected cards that supported the deck’s identity
- Final recommendations were framed as test swaps, not commands

This is the intended spirit of The Dragon’s Touch: help the player make clearer decisions without taking ownership of the deck away from them.

## Playtest Questions

After testing the suggested package, the pilot should consider:

1. Does the deck still feel like a Dog/corgi deck?
2. Does Phelia feel like the center of the deck more often?
3. Are the flicker-control cards showing up and mattering?
4. Does Hanged Executioner provide useful interaction or token support?
5. Does Odric help turn wide boards into real threats?
6. Does Ajani help create the giant Corgi win condition?
7. Was cutting Sisay’s Ring noticeable in a bad way, or did the deck feel smoother?
8. Did the removed interaction spells feel missed?
9. Does Panharmonicon feel worth keeping after actual games?
10. Does the deck stay within the intended Bracket 2 casual/core feel?

## Transparency Note

The suggested swaps are not commands.

They are not a claim that the deck is solved, optimized, or perfect. They are a small test package based on the deck’s stated goals, corrected strategy, collection-only restriction, and casual/core table target.

The pilot should playtest, adjust, and keep the final decision in their own hands.
